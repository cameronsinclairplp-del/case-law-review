# Woolmington v DPP [1935] AC 462 (HL)

## One line
The “golden thread” of the criminal law — the prosecution must prove the accused’s guilt; the accused need not prove innocence (subject only to insanity and statutory exceptions).

## What happened
Reginald WOOLMINGTON, aged 21, was charged with murdering his estranged 17-year-old wife. He claimed the killing was an accident — that he only meant to frighten her into returning to him by threatening suicide with a gun, but it went off. The trial judge directed the jury that, once the killing was proved, it was for WOOLMINGTON to prove it was an accident. He was convicted and sentenced to death; the Court of Criminal Appeal upheld the conviction; the House of Lords quashed it.

## What the Court held
Viscount SANKEY LC stated the “golden thread”: it is the duty of the prosecution to prove the prisoner’s guilt, subject only to the defence of insanity and to any statutory exception. If, on the whole of the case, there is a reasonable doubt — raised by either the prosecution’s or the prisoner’s evidence — the prosecution has not made out its case and the accused is entitled to an acquittal. The accused never has to prove innocence.

## What it means for your casework
This is the cornerstone you build every brief on: the State proves guilt — the suspect proves nothing. Assemble your evidence to discharge that burden to the criminal standard, and never structure a case (or an interview) on the premise that a suspect must explain themselves or account for their innocence. The main carve-out you’ll meet on the job is insanity / unsoundness of mind, which the accused must raise and prove (see Stefanski), plus specific statutory reverse-onus provisions.

## Verdict
AWARENESS — the foundational statement of the prosecution’s burden; it shapes how you build and think about every brief, even though it lays down no operational procedure.

## Full judgment (source text - https://www.bailii.org/uk/cases/UKHL/1935/1.html)

Leading passage only — the verbatim “golden thread” ratio of Viscount SANKEY LC. The full speech ([1935] AC 462) is to be added when available.

HOLDING / RATIO — THE "GOLDEN THREAD"

Throughout the web of the English Criminal Law one golden thread is always to be seen, that it is the duty of the prosecution to prove the prisoner's guilt subject to [what I have already said as to] the defence of insanity and subject also to any statutory exception. If, at the end of and on the whole of the case, there is a reasonable doubt, created by the evidence given by either the prosecution or the prisoner, [...] the prosecution has not made out the case and the prisoner is entitled to an acquittal. No matter what the charge or where the trial, the principle that the prosecution must prove the guilt of the prisoner is part of the common law of England and no attempt to whittle it down can be entertained.
